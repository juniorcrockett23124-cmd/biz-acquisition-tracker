# Research Memo - $400,000 Revenue NE Hartford CT Area CPA Practice

## Core Thesis

This is a credible lower-middle-market CPA tuck-in or first platform candidate if the bookkeeping and consulting work is material and the two staff members genuinely anchor delivery, but it drops quickly in quality if the business is mostly seasonal tax prep wrapped in optimistic growth language.

## Market And Industry Notes

The accounting sector remains attractive when recurring work, client trust, and local reputation are real, but weaker transactional bookkeeping continues to face automation pressure. BLS projects accountants and auditors to grow 5% from 2024 to 2034 while bookkeeping clerk roles decline 6%, which means firms with stronger advisory, bookkeeping oversight, and business-client relationships should age better than return-only practices. AICPA's succession commentary also suggests many small firms still struggle with staffing and handoffs, which supports acquisition supply but increases the importance of people diligence. In a Hartford-area practice, the key issue is not whether there is demand for CPA work; it is whether enough of that demand is recurring and team-deliverable.

## What Is Attractive

- The valuation is reasonable relative to the reported cash flow.
- Recent 15% to 25% annual growth is encouraging if it is organic and retention-backed.
- Two staff expected to transition is a meaningful plus for a firm of this size.
- Tax, bookkeeping, and consulting together can create more year-round customer touchpoints than tax-only work.
- Retirement motivation is a favorable seller signal.

## What Is Dangerous

- Growth can look impressive in a small practice without actually proving durable systems or strong retention.
- Moderate recurring-revenue quality suggests the business may still be meaningfully tax-season weighted.
- Two staff may not be enough if the owner still performs most review, client management, or business development.
- Consulting revenue can be less repeatable than bookkeeping or payroll if it is project-driven.
- Small practices often lack robust SOPs, leaving a buyer to rebuild process discipline after close.

## Must-Ask Diligence Questions

- What share of revenue is monthly or quarterly recurring versus annual tax-season work?
- What exactly drove the recent growth: new clients, pricing, add-on services, or one-off consulting?
- What do the two staff members do today, how long have they been with the firm, and which clients know them directly?
- What is the top-client concentration and what percent of revenue comes from business clients versus 1040 work?
- What review, sign-off, and relationship responsibilities stay with the owner today?
- Will the seller remain through one full tax season to support retention and workflow transfer?
