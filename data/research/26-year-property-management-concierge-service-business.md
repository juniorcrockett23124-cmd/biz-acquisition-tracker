# Research Memo - 26-Year Property Management & Concierge Service Business

## Core Thesis

This is attractive only if it is truly a contract-backed homeowner-services platform rather than a high-end personal-relationship job wrapped in recurring-revenue language.

## Market And Industry Notes

Aspen is a structurally strong luxury destination, which supports demand for absentee-owner oversight, inspections, and concierge support. That said, the operating environment is regulation-heavy: City of Aspen short-term rentals require permits and licensing, and city tax and permit rules continue to matter for owners who rent homes for stays under 30 days. The business may benefit from that complexity if owners prefer outsourcing compliance and oversight, but regulatory friction can also cap client growth and raise service expectations. This is not a generic property-management market; it is a small, premium, relationship-driven niche with high service standards.

## What Is Attractive

- Twenty-six years of operating history and strong reputation are meaningful in a referral-based luxury market.
- Approximately 20 homes can be enough for a good lifestyle cash-flow business if relationships are sticky and pricing is disciplined.
- The asking price versus SDE is reasonable by small-business standards, especially with seller financing indicated.
- Low payroll intensity can support margins if subcontractors are reliable and clients are accustomed to the model.

## What Is Dangerous

- Client concentration is extreme by definition: losing only a few homes could impair earnings materially.
- Aspen short-term-rental permits, taxes, and local compliance rules create ongoing regulatory exposure for owners and their managers.
- The concierge element can conceal a business that depends on the founder's taste, responsiveness, and trusted local vendor network.
- Subcontractor dependence can make service quality uneven during peak seasons or emergency events.
- Growth is not obviously linear; in a premium market, adding homes can also add complexity, not just revenue.

## Must-Ask Diligence Questions

- How much revenue comes from fixed management contracts versus ad hoc concierge requests?
- What percent of gross profit is tied to the top five homes?
- Which vendor relationships are exclusive or founder-personal rather than company-owned?
- Who handles after-hours emergencies, owner communication, and local compliance coordination today?
- How many homes are used as short-term rentals and therefore exposed to permit and tax rule changes?
- Are subcontractors locked in with agreements, or can they walk post-close?
