# Research Memo - CPA Firm Chicago

## Core Thesis

This looks like a promising small CPA acquisition because the listing combines recurring monthly work, staying staff, and retirement motivation, but the real value depends on whether client relationships and technical review authority already extend beyond the owner.

## Market And Industry Notes

The industry backdrop remains favorable for quality accounting practices with recurring client service. BLS projects accountants and auditors employment to grow 5% from 2024 to 2034, while bookkeeping, accounting, and auditing clerk employment is projected to decline 6%, reinforcing the split between higher-value advisory and compliance work versus lower-value transaction processing. AICPA's recent small-firm commentary continues to flag staffing and succession as core issues for firms. That is good for buying opportunities because older owners need exits, but it also means team retention and workflow modernization matter more than ever. A small Chicago-area practice can still be attractive if its monthly bookkeeping and write-up work is sticky enough to smooth the tax-season cycle.

## What Is Attractive

- More than 40% monthly recurring revenue is a meaningful positive signal for transferability.
- Staff expected to remain reduces the risk of buying a pure owner-only client book.
- The near-west-suburb location should support a stable SMB and household tax client base.
- A 62% profit margin suggests either efficient operations or under-market owner compensation, both worth investigating.
- Retirement motivation is exactly the type of seller signal the buy box prefers.

## What Is Dangerous

- Years in business are not disclosed, which leaves too much uncertainty around historical client retention and brand durability.
- Small firms can appear recurring on paper while still relying on the owner for final review, issue resolution, and top-client trust.
- If the revenue mix is still tax-heavy, the practice may be less predictable than the monthly-work language suggests.
- Staff continuity risk is real if compensation is light, responsibilities are fuzzy, or sale communication is mishandled.
- Pricing pressure is rising on basic bookkeeping while client expectations are rising around portals, responsiveness, and advisory support.

## Must-Ask Diligence Questions

- What percent of revenue comes from monthly bookkeeping/write-up, payroll, annual tax, and advisory work?
- How long has the firm operated under the current brand, and what has revenue retention looked like over the last three years?
- Which staff members are licensed, who reviews work today, and which client relationships are truly shared?
- What are the top 10 clients as a percent of revenue, and how many clients generate the first 50% of billings?
- What software stack, portal workflow, and SOPs support the recurring work?
- Will the seller stay through one full tax cycle, and is seller carry available?
