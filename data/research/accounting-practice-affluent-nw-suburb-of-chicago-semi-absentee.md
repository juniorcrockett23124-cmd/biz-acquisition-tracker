# Research Memo - Accounting practice affluent NW suburb of Chicago, Semi-Absentee

## Core Thesis

This is a high-quality small-firm accounting platform candidate because the client book is diversified across tax, monthly/quarterly work, and consulting, the owner appears partially removed already, and retirement plus a long transition offer materially reduce handoff risk.

## Market And Industry Notes

Chicago-area accounting demand should remain durable because tax compliance and controller-style support are still recurring needs for small businesses, even as AI automates lower-value bookkeeping tasks. The better signal here is that advisory-capable firms with stable staff are becoming scarcer, not more common: BLS still projects 124,200 accountant and auditor openings per year over 2024-2034, and AICPA continues to flag staffing, succession, and AI adaptation as top small-firm issues. That environment helps retention of a good client base, but it also means replacing senior staff and review talent will stay expensive.

## What Is Attractive

- The revenue mix is healthier than a pure tax shop: 45% tax, 30% recurring monthly or quarterly billings, and 25% other work.
- Six full-time employees for a 39-year-old firm is the clearest current evidence of real operating infrastructure.
- Owner works about 20 hours per week and takes substantial vacation, which supports the semi-absentee claim more than most listings.
- Retirement is explicit, and the seller is willing to stay on up to 20 hours per week for a year or longer.
- Lease expense looks very manageable relative to cash flow, which preserves margin.

## What Is Dangerous

- The listing explicitly says the buyer should have tax preparation experience, which suggests technical review, client trust, or compliance signoff may still hinge on licensed expertise.
- Staffing scarcity in public accounting raises the downside if even one key preparer or reviewer leaves after closing.
- The 25% "other" category could hide project work, one-time cleanup engagements, or seller-led advisory that is less durable than the headline recurring profile.
- Client concentration and age mix are still unknown; if a meaningful share of the book is aging individual tax clients, attrition could accelerate.

## Must-Ask Diligence Questions

- How much of revenue is under recurring monthly agreements versus annual tax-season work?
- Which employees hold CPA or EA credentials, and who currently signs off on reviews?
- What percent of billings comes from the top 10 clients, and what was client attrition in 2023, 2024, and 2025?
- How many client relationships are owned by staff versus the seller personally?
- What exactly sits inside the 25% "other" bucket?
- Is seller financing available, and are there retention-based protections tied to transition?
